The Orient Express

It is 1897 and Mme. DuVerre, stares out of her window at a tall, gilded lamppost that bears a clock informing her that it is 3:25pm in the afternoon. Almost time for high tea. She looks on at the signs hanging by the other side of the post and reads one that says Istanbul, and the other, something in Russian. Probably Istanbul she confesses. Standing by the post are three rather tall, very well dressed gentlemen who share similar taste in their sac suits. All cut from the same cloth, single-breasted, and four buttons with double topstitched edges. Each stands with luggage in his hand – a Gladstone bag, a square suitcase and a round hatbox. The fresh tan leather with thick orange stitching gives away the sole identity

of the craftsman. They’re probably waiting on their Madame to board. Time for my tea.

After 47 hours on the Orient Express, Mme. DuVerre knows the way from her carriage to the Restaurant Coach. She opens the door and is greeted by fine crystal lights shaped like tulips lined up against the walls of the coach. She smoothens her hand over the fine silk pointelle tablecloth and delicately touches the silverware laid with absolute perfection. I couldn’t have done it better myself. She takes a seat, assuring that her perfectly sculpted dress embellished with the finest Edwardian lace and French chiffon will remain safe for the duration of her meal.
